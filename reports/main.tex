\section{Abstract} \label{sec:abstract}

This report presents the results of a free-flight supersonic cone experiment conducted in the University of Maryland Ludwieg tube facility. The experiment aimed to characterize freestream flow conditions, measure cone geometry from shadowgraph imagery, determine drag forces from free-flight kinematics, and validate theoretical drag predictions based on Rasmussen's approximations for conical flow. The test section flow was established at Mach $M_\infty = 3.79$ with static conditions $p_\infty = \qty{611}{Pa}$ and $T_\infty = \qty{76.4}{K}$. Automated edge detection applied to 193 high-speed shadowgraph frames captured the trajectory of an $\theta_c = 6.98^\circ$ half-angle cone with base radius $r = \qty{4.88}{mm}$. Quadratic regression of the vertex position versus time yielded a constant deceleration of $a_x = \qty{50.0}{m/s^2}$, corresponding to an experimental drag coefficient of $C_D = 0.196$. This value is 43\% lower than the theoretical inviscid prediction of $C_D = 0.344$ from Rasmussen's pressure coefficient approximation, resulting in an impossible negative viscous drag component. The discrepancy indicates either systematic errors in the optical geometry measurements (particularly the cone base area determination from two-dimensional projections) or limitations in the analytical approximations at the tested flow conditions. Despite this anomaly, the experiment successfully demonstrated fundamental free-flight testing principles and highlighted the critical importance of independent dimensional verification and computational validation for supersonic aerodynamic measurements.

\section{Introduction} \label{sec:introduction}

The Ludwieg tube is a type of impulse wind tunnel capable of generating high-speed supersonic and hypersonic flows efficiently and cost-effectively, albeit with limited test duration \cite{slaurence2026ludwieg}. Unlike continuous-flow facilities that require large compressors and extensive power infrastructure, the Ludwieg tube operates on a simple principle: a long charge tube is pressurized to the desired fill conditions, then a fast-acting valve or burst diaphragm is opened, allowing an unsteady expansion wave to propagate upstream while accelerating the gas through a converging-diverging nozzle into the test section. This transient operation provides clean, uniform flow conditions ideal for fundamental aerodynamic studies, though the steady test time is typically limited to tens of milliseconds.

When a body travels through a supersonic or hypersonic flow, the fluid cannot be compressed isentropically ahead of the body due to information traveling only at the local sound speed. Instead, discontinuous shock waves form to rapidly compress the flow and redirect it around the obstacle \cite{danderson2023fundamentals}. For slender axisymmetric bodies such as cones, an attached oblique shock wave emanates from the apex, creating a conical shock surface. A remarkable property of supersonic conical flow is that all flow properties along any ray drawn from the cone vertex remain constant, though they vary from ray to ray as the flow is further compressed between the shock and the body surface \cite{slaurence2026ludwieg}. This flow structure is fundamentally different from two-dimensional wedge flows and requires solving the Taylor-Maccoll ordinary differential equation for exact solutions.

Free-flying models offer significant advantages over traditional sting-mounted configurations in wind tunnel testing. Sting supports can introduce flow interference effects that contaminate force measurements, particularly for drag-sensitive geometries or base pressure studies. By eliminating physical support structures, free-flying experiments provide cleaner aerodynamic measurements. However, this approach introduces its own challenges: the model's motion must be tracked with high precision using non-intrusive optical methods, and the short test duration of impulse facilities limits the time available for meaningful acceleration measurements. Recent advances in high-speed digital imaging and automated image processing have made free-flight techniques increasingly practical for quantitative aerodynamic force determination.

\subsection{Theoretical Background} \label{sec:introduction/theory}

\subsubsection{Ludwieg Tube Flow Analysis}

The flow development in a Ludwieg tube involves a complex interaction between unsteady expansion waves and steady isentropic nozzle flow. When the fast-acting valve opens, an expansion wave propagates upstream into the quiescent charge tube at approximately the local sound speed. This expansion accelerates the gas to a subsonic Mach number $M_t$ determined by the area ratio between the charge tube and nozzle throat according to the area-Mach number relationship:

\begin{equation}
    \left(\frac{A_t}{A^*}\right)^2 = \frac{1}{M_t^2} \left[\frac{2}{\gamma + 1}\left(1 + \frac{\gamma - 1}{2}M_t^2\right)\right]^{(\gamma+1)/(\gamma-1)}
    \label{eq:area-mach-tube}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $A_t$ is the charge tube cross-sectional area, $A^*$ is the nozzle throat area, and $\gamma$ is the specific heat ratio. For this equation, the subsonic solution for $M_t$ must be selected.

The pressure and temperature behind the expansion wave, $p_t$ and $T_t$, are related to the original fill conditions by:

\begin{align}
    \frac{p_t}{p_{\text{fill}}} &= \left(1 + \frac{\gamma - 1}{2}M_t^2\right)^{-2\gamma/(\gamma-1)} \label{eq:expansion-pressure} \\
    \frac{T_t}{T_{\text{fill}}} &= \left(1 + \frac{\gamma - 1}{2}M_t^2\right)^{-2} \label{eq:expansion-temperature}
\end{align}

These post-expansion conditions serve as the effective reservoir conditions for the subsequent isentropic expansion through the nozzle. The stagnation pressure and temperature are then:

\begin{align}
    \frac{p_0}{p_t} &= \left(1 + \frac{\gamma - 1}{2}M_t^2\right)^{\gamma/(\gamma-1)} \label{eq:stagnation-pressure} \\
    \frac{T_0}{T_t} &= 1 + \frac{\gamma - 1}{2}M_t^2 \label{eq:stagnation-temperature}
\end{align}

Finally, the freestream static conditions in the test section are determined from the calibrated exit Mach number $M_\infty$ using the isentropic relations:

\begin{align}
    \frac{p_0}{p_\infty} &= \left(1 + \frac{\gamma - 1}{2}M_\infty^2\right)^{\gamma/(\gamma-1)} \label{eq:freestream-pressure} \\
    \frac{T_0}{T_\infty} &= 1 + \frac{\gamma - 1}{2}M_\infty^2 \label{eq:freestream-temperature}
\end{align}

The freestream density and velocity then follow from the ideal gas law and definition of Mach number: $\rho_\infty = p_\infty/(RT_\infty)$ and $V_\infty = M_\infty\sqrt{\gamma R T_\infty}$.

\subsubsection{Supersonic Conical Flow Theory}

The exact solution for supersonic flow over a cone requires solving the Taylor-Maccoll equation, a nonlinear ordinary differential equation with no closed-form analytical solution. Numerical integration is therefore required to obtain the flowfield properties and surface pressure distribution. However, Rasmussen developed accurate analytical approximations that avoid this computational complexity while maintaining excellent agreement with full numerical solutions for slender cones at high Mach numbers \cite{slaurence2026ludwieg}.

For the shock angle $\beta$ (measured from the freestream direction), Rasmussen's approximation is:

\begin{equation}
    \frac{\sin\beta}{\sin\theta_c} \approx \sqrt{\frac{\gamma + 1}{2} + \frac{1}{M_\infty^2\sin^2\theta_c}}
    \label{eq:rasmussen-shock}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\theta_c$ is the cone half-angle. For the pressure coefficient on the cone surface, the approximation is:

\begin{equation}
    \frac{C_p}{\theta_c^2} \approx 1 + \frac{(\gamma + 1)M_\infty^2}{\theta_c^2 + 2} + \frac{(\gamma - 1)M_\infty^2}{\theta_c^2 + 2}\ln\left(\frac{\gamma + 1}{2} + \frac{1}{M_\infty^2\theta_c^2}\right)
    \label{eq:rasmussen-cp}
\end{equation}

An important property of slender conical flows is that the pressure coefficient $C_p$ is approximately uniform over the entire cone surface. Consequently, for inviscid flow, $C_p$ is equal to the inviscid drag coefficient $C_{D,\text{inviscid}}$.

\subsubsection{Drag Force Determination}

For a free-flying body subject only to aerodynamic drag, Newton's second law gives $D = ma_x$, where $D$ is the drag force, $m$ is the body mass, and $a_x$ is the acceleration in the flow direction. If the cone's position is tracked over time using high-speed imaging, a quadratic polynomial can be fitted to the position data:

\begin{equation}
    x(t) = a_0 + a_1 t + a_2 t^2
    \label{eq:quadratic-fit}
\end{equation}

The constant acceleration is then simply $a_x = 2a_2$. The drag coefficient is defined as:

\begin{equation}
    C_D \equiv \frac{2D}{\rho_\infty V_\infty^2 A}
    \label{eq:drag-coefficient}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $A$ is the reference area (cone base area, $A = \pi r^2$). Comparison between the experimentally-determined $C_D$ and the inviscid theoretical prediction from Rasmussen's $C_p$ approximation allows estimation of the viscous drag component, which arises from skin friction and boundary layer displacement effects.

\subsection{Experiment Objectives} \label{sec:introduction/objectives}

The specific objectives of this experiment are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Determine the freestream flow conditions (Mach number, pressure, temperature, density, and velocity) in the Ludwieg tube test section from the measured fill conditions using isentropic flow relations and expansion wave theory.

    \item Track the position of a free-flying cone over time using high-speed shadowgraph imaging at \qty{10}{kHz} frame rate and automated edge detection algorithms.

    \item Calculate the experimental drag coefficient from the measured cone acceleration by fitting a quadratic polynomial to the position-time trajectory and applying Newton's second law.

    \item Compute the theoretical inviscid drag coefficient using Rasmussen's pressure coefficient approximation for supersonic conical flow.

    \item Visualize the predicted shock wave geometry and compare the theoretical shock angle with the cone geometry observed in experimental images.

    \item Estimate the viscous drag component (skin friction and boundary layer effects) by comparing the experimental drag coefficient with the inviscid theoretical prediction.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Methodology} \label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{Experimental Apparatus} \label{sec:methodology/apparatus}

The experiment was conducted in the University of Maryland Mach-4 Ludwieg tube, an unheated impulse wind tunnel facility. The apparatus consists of a long charge tube connected to a converging-diverging nozzle through a fast-acting valve, as shown in \cref{fig:methodology/apparatus/ludweig-tube}. The charge tube has a cross-sectional area-to-throat area ratio of $A_t/A^* = 11.4$. For this particular test, the experimentally measured nozzle exit Mach number was $M_\infty = 3.79$.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.65\linewidth]{../assets/ludweig-tube.png}
    \caption{Schematic Diagram of a Ludwieg Tube \cite{slaurence2026ludwieg}.}
    \label{fig:methodology/apparatus/ludweig-tube}
\end{figure}

The test article was a slender cone fabricated from lightweight material with a mass of \qty{1.8}{g}. The cone was configured as a free-flying model without any physical support structures (sting-free) to eliminate potential flow interference effects on drag measurements. The cone half-angle $\theta_c$ and base radius $r$ were determined from the edge detection analysis of experimental images, as detailed in \cref{sec:results/drag/experimental}. High-speed shadowgraph imaging was employed to visualize the cone and surrounding flowfield, with images captured at a frame rate of \qty{10}{kHz} (\qty{0.1}{ms} temporal resolution). The imaging system was calibrated to provide a spatial resolution of \qty{12.6}{pixels/mm}, enabling sub-millimeter position tracking of the cone vertex throughout its trajectory.

\subsection{Operating Procedure} \label{sec:methodology/procedure}

The experimental procedure was as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The charge tube was filled with air to a pressure of $p_{\text{fill}} = \qty{0.7}{bar}$ (\qty{70}{kPa}) at ambient temperature $T_{\text{fill}} = \qty{296}{K}$.

    \item The cone was positioned in the test section on a release mechanism that would deploy it into the freestream upon flow establishment.

    \item The high-speed shadowgraph imaging system was configured for \qty{10}{kHz} frame rate, providing \qty{0.1}{ms} time resolution between successive images.

    \item The fast-acting valve was opened, initiating an unsteady expansion wave that propagated upstream through the charge tube while accelerating the flow through the converging-diverging nozzle.

    \item Once steady flow conditions were established in the test section (approximately \qtyrange{5}{10}{ms} after valve opening), the cone was released and began its free-flight trajectory.

    \item A total of \num{193} frames were captured during the steady flow period, documenting the cone's position and orientation as it accelerated downstream under the influence of aerodynamic drag.

    \item The experimental images were post-processed using an automated Python-based edge detection algorithm (ported from the original MATLAB implementation) to extract the upper and lower cone edge coordinates in each frame.

    \item Cone vertex positions were determined by fitting linear regressions to the detected edges and computing their intersection point, enabling sub-pixel position accuracy for drag force determination.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Results} \label{sec:results}

\subsection{Freestream Flow Conditions} \label{sec:results/freestream}

The freestream flow conditions in the test section were determined from the measured fill conditions using the equations presented in \ref{sec:introduction/theory}. The experimental parameters were:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Fill pressure: $p_{\text{fill}} = \qty{0.7}{bar} = \qty{70000}{Pa}$
    \item Fill temperature: $T_{\text{fill}} = \qty{296}{K}$
    \item Charge tube area ratio: $A_t/A^* = 11.4$
    \item Calibrated exit Mach number: $M_\infty = 4.07$
    \item Specific heat ratio (air): $\gamma = 1.4$
    \item Gas constant (air): $R = \qty{287}{J/(kg.K)}$
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Step 1: Charge Tube Mach Number}

Solving \cref{eq:area-mach-tube} numerically for the subsonic solution yields the Mach number in the charge tube behind the expansion wave:

\begin{equation}
    M_t = 0.0508
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Step 2: Post-Expansion Conditions}

Using \cref{eq:expansion-pressure,eq:expansion-temperature}, the pressure and temperature behind the expansion wave are:

\begin{align}
    p_t &= p_{\text{fill}} \left(1 + \frac{\gamma - 1}{2}M_t^2\right)^{-2\gamma/(\gamma-1)} = \qty{69747}{Pa} \\
    T_t &= T_{\text{fill}} \left(1 + \frac{\gamma - 1}{2}M_t^2\right)^{-2} = \qty{295.7}{K}
\end{align}

\subsubsection{Step 3: Stagnation Conditions}

Applying \cref{eq:stagnation-pressure,eq:stagnation-temperature}, the stagnation conditions are:

\begin{align}
    p_0 &= p_t \left(1 + \frac{\gamma - 1}{2}M_t^2\right)^{\gamma/(\gamma-1)} = \qty{69873}{Pa} \\
    T_0 &= T_t \left(1 + \frac{\gamma - 1}{2}M_t^2\right) = \qty{295.8}{K}
\end{align}

\subsubsection{Step 4: Freestream Static Conditions}

Finally, using the experimentally measured exit Mach number $M_\infty = 3.79$ and \cref{eq:freestream-pressure,eq:freestream-temperature}, the freestream static conditions are:

\begin{align}
    p_\infty &= \frac{p_0}{\left(1 + \frac{\gamma - 1}{2}M_\infty^2\right)^{\gamma/(\gamma-1)}} = \qty{611}{Pa} \\
    T_\infty &= \frac{T_0}{1 + \frac{\gamma - 1}{2}M_\infty^2} = \qty{76.4}{K} \\
    \rho_\infty &= \frac{p_\infty}{RT_\infty} = \qty{0.0279}{kg/m^3} \\
    a_\infty &= \sqrt{\gamma R T_\infty} = \qty{175}{m/s} \\
    V_\infty &= M_\infty a_\infty = \qty{664}{m/s}
\end{align}

These calculated freestream conditions provide the reference state for all subsequent drag coefficient and Reynolds number calculations.  The complete results are summarized in \cref{tab:results/freestream/summary}.

\subsection{Drag} \label{sec:results/drag}

\subsubsection{Theoretical} \label{sec:results/drag/theoretical}

The theoretical inviscid drag coefficient was calculated using Rasmussen's pressure coefficient approximation (\ref{eq:rasmussen-cp}). The required cone geometry parameters---half-angle $\theta_c$ and base radius $r$---were extracted from the edge detection analysis of the experimental images, as described in \ref{sec:results/drag/experimental}.

\paragraph{Cone Geometry}

From the median cone angle across all \num{193} frames and the initial base diameter from the first 20 frames (to minimize perspective effects as the cone recedes):

\begin{align}
    \theta_c &= 0.122 \text{ rad} = 6.98^\circ \\
    r &= \qty{4.88}{mm} = \qty{0.00488}{m} \\
    A &= \pi r^2 = \qty{7.48e-5}{m^2}
\end{align}

\paragraph{Rasmussen Pressure Coefficient}

Applying \cref{eq:rasmussen-cp} with $M_\infty = 3.79$ and the measured cone half-angle:

\begin{equation}
    C_p = \theta_c^2 \left[1 + \frac{(\gamma + 1)M_\infty^2}{\theta_c^2 + 2} + \frac{(\gamma - 1)M_\infty^2}{\theta_c^2 + 2}\ln\left(\frac{\gamma + 1}{2} + \frac{1}{M_\infty^2\theta_c^2}\right)\right] = 0.344
\end{equation}

For slender conical flows, the surface pressure coefficient is uniform and equals the inviscid drag coefficient:

\begin{equation}
    C_{D,\text{inviscid}} = C_p = 0.344
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Theoretical Drag Force and Acceleration}

Using the calculated freestream dynamic pressure $q_\infty = \frac{1}{2}\rho_\infty V_\infty^2 = \qty{6146}{Pa}$ and cone base area:

\begin{align}
    D_{\text{inviscid}} &= C_{D,\text{inviscid}} \cdot q_\infty \cdot A = \qty{0.158}{N} \\
    a_{\text{inviscid}} &= \frac{D_{\text{inviscid}}}{m} = \frac{\qty{0.158}{N}}{\qty{0.0018}{kg}} = \qty{87.9}{m/s^2}
\end{align}

This theoretical prediction assumes inviscid flow and provides a baseline for comparison with the experimental drag measurement.

\subsubsection{Experimental} \label{sec:results/drag/experimental}

The experimental drag coefficient was determined from the free-flight kinematics of the cone model in the test section. The analysis utilized automated edge detection applied to 193 sequential shadowgraph frames captured at \qty{10}{kHz}, spanning approximately \qty{19.3}{ms} of flight time. For each frame, the upper and lower edges of the cone were extracted and fitted with linear functions to determine the cone orientation and vertex position. The vertex $x$-coordinate in each frame provided a time-resolved trajectory of the cone motion.

The trajectory data $x(t)$ was fitted with a quadratic polynomial to extract the constant acceleration due to drag force:

\begin{equation}
    x(t) = a_0 + a_1 t + a_2 t^2
\end{equation}

The least-squares fit yielded coefficients $a_0 = \qty{0.0204}{m}$, $a_1 = \qty{1.016}{m/s}$, and $a_2 = \qty{25.01}{m/s^2}$. The position, velocity, and acceleration profiles are shown in \cref{fig:results/drag/trajectory-position,fig:results/drag/trajectory-velocity,fig:results/drag/trajectory-acceleration}.

The constant deceleration of the cone is given by $a_x = 2a_2 = \qty{50.02}{m/s^2}$. Using Newton's second law with the measured cone mass $m = \qty{1.8}{g} = \qty{0.0018}{kg}$, the time-averaged drag force is:

\begin{equation}
    D = m \cdot a_x = \qty{0.0018}{kg} \times \qty{50.02}{m/s^2} = \qty{0.090}{N}
\end{equation}

The experimental drag coefficient is then calculated from the drag equation evaluated at the freestream conditions:

\begin{equation}
    C_D = \frac{2D}{\rho_\infty V_\infty^2 A} = \frac{2 \times \qty{0.090}{N}}{(\qty{0.0279}{kg/m^3})(\qty{664}{m/s})^2(\qty{7.48e-5}{m^2})} = 0.196
\end{equation}

where $A = \pi r^2$ is the cone base area. This experimental value represents the total drag coefficient, including both pressure (inviscid) and viscous (skin friction) contributions.

\subsection{Shock Angle} \label{sec:results/shock-angle}

The theoretical shock angle was calculated using Rasmussen's approximation (\cref{eq:rasmussen-shock}) for the measured cone half-angle and freestream Mach number. Applying this relationship:

\begin{equation}
    \sin\beta = \sin\theta_c \sqrt{\frac{\gamma + 1}{2} + \frac{1}{M_\infty^2\sin^2\theta_c}}
\end{equation}

With $M_\infty = 3.79$, $\theta_c = 0.122$ rad, and $\gamma = 1.4$, the shock angle is:

\begin{equation}
    \beta = 0.300 \text{ rad} = 17.2^\circ
\end{equation}

The shock wave forms a conical surface emanating from the cone vertex. In a two-dimensional side view (as captured in the shadowgraph images), this conical shock appears as two straight rays at angle $\beta$ measured from the freestream direction. The theoretical shock geometry is visualized in \cref{fig:results/shock-angle/theoretical-shock}, where the predicted shock rays (red dashed lines) are overlaid on the detected cone edges (blue solid lines) from a representative experimental frame.

The shock angle $\beta$ is consistently larger than the cone surface angle $\theta_c$, as required for attached shock conditions. The flow between the shock and cone surface undergoes additional compression and turning to become parallel to the cone surface. While the experimental shadowgraph images do not show clearly visible shock waves due to the relatively low density in this Mach-4 flow, the theoretical prediction provides the expected shock geometry for validation against computational simulations or future high-sensitivity visualization experiments.

\section{Analysis and Discussion} \label{sec:analysis}

\subsection{Comparison Between Theoretical and Experimental Drag Effects} \label{sec:analysis/comparison}

A direct comparison between the theoretical inviscid drag prediction and the experimental measurement reveals a significant discrepancy. \Cref{tab:analysis/comparison/drag-summary} summarizes the key drag parameters obtained from both approaches.

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \caption{Comparison of theoretical and experimental drag parameters.}
    \label{tab:analysis/comparison/drag-summary}
    \begin{tabular}{lcc}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Parameter} & \textbf{Theoretical (Inviscid)} & \textbf{Experimental} \\
        \midrule
        Drag coefficient, $C_D$ & 0.344 & 0.196 \\
        Drag force, $D$ [N] & 0.158 & 0.090 \\
        Acceleration, $a_x$ [m/s$^2$] & 87.9 & 50.0 \\
        Ratio $C_{D,\text{exp}}/C_{D,\text{theory}}$ & --- & 0.57 \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The experimental drag coefficient of $C_D = 0.196$ is substantially lower than the theoretical inviscid prediction of $C_D = 0.344$ based on Rasmussen's approximation. The experimental value is only 57\% of the theoretical prediction, corresponding to a 43\% reduction in drag. This result is physically inconsistent with expectations for supersonic cone flow, where viscous effects (boundary layer skin friction) should \textit{increase} the total drag above the inviscid pressure drag baseline, not reduce it.

Several potential sources of this discrepancy warrant investigation:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Rasmussen approximation accuracy:} The Rasmussen formulas (\cref{eq:rasmussen-shock,eq:rasmussen-cp}) are analytical approximations valid for slender cones at high Mach numbers. At the experimental conditions ($M_\infty = 3.79$, $\theta_c = 6.98^\circ$), these approximations may overestimate the pressure coefficient. More accurate predictions could be obtained from Taylor-Maccoll solutions or CFD simulations.

    \item \textbf{Three-dimensional perspective effects:} The cone base radius was measured from two-dimensional shadowgraph projections. As the cone recedes from the camera during flight, perspective foreshortening could introduce systematic errors in the base area determination, even with the corrections applied (using initial frames only). An overestimated base area would lead to an underestimated experimental $C_D$.

    \item \textbf{Flow unsteadiness and startup effects:} The Ludwieg tube produces nominally steady flow, but expansion wave reflections and boundary layer growth in the test section could cause temporal variations in freestream conditions. The constant-acceleration assumption may not fully capture transient effects during the cone's flight.

    \item \textbf{Base drag assumptions:} The theoretical drag calculation assumes that the base pressure equals the freestream static pressure. In reality, the separated flow behind the blunt base creates a low-pressure recirculation zone that contributes additional base drag. However, this effect would \textit{increase} the experimental drag, not decrease it, making the discrepancy more severe.
\end{enumerate}

\Cref{fig:analysis/comparison/drag-coefficient} provides a visual comparison of the theoretical and experimental drag coefficients. The substantial gap between these values indicates that further investigation is needed to identify the root cause of the discrepancy. Future work should include independent verification of the cone geometry, CFD validation of the Rasmussen approximations at these flow conditions, and high-speed pressure measurements on the cone surface.

\subsection{Viscous Component of Drag} \label{sec:analysis/viscous}

In principle, the viscous drag component can be estimated by subtracting the inviscid pressure drag from the total experimental drag:

\begin{equation}
    C_{D,\text{viscous}} = C_{D,\text{experimental}} - C_{D,\text{inviscid}}
\end{equation}

Applying this decomposition to the measured values yields:

\begin{equation}
    C_{D,\text{viscous}} = 0.196 - 0.344 = -0.148
\end{equation}

This negative viscous drag coefficient is physically impossible. Viscous effects in supersonic boundary layers always produce positive skin friction drag that adds to the inviscid pressure drag. A negative value would imply that viscosity somehow reduces the total drag below the inviscid baseline, violating fundamental principles of fluid mechanics.

The impossible viscous drag result confirms that the discrepancy identified in \cref{sec:analysis/comparison} cannot be attributed to viscous effects. Instead, it indicates one or more of the following:

\begin{itemize}
    \item The theoretical inviscid drag prediction from Rasmussen's approximation significantly overestimates the actual inviscid drag at these flow conditions
    \item Systematic errors in the experimental measurement (cone mass, base area, trajectory analysis) have underestimated the true drag force
    \item The assumptions underlying the analysis (constant freestream conditions, two-dimensional geometry extraction, constant deceleration) introduce compounding uncertainties
\end{itemize}

For reference, typical skin friction drag on slender cones at supersonic speeds contributes 10--20\% of the total drag for moderate Reynolds numbers. At the experimental conditions ($\text{Re}_L \approx 10^5$ based on cone length), a positive viscous contribution of $C_{D,\text{viscous}} \approx 0.03$--$0.05$ would be expected, bringing the total drag coefficient to approximately $C_{D,\text{total}} \approx 0.37$--$0.39$. This is nearly double the experimentally measured value of $C_D = 0.196$.

Given the magnitude and direction of this discrepancy, we recommend that future experiments incorporate the following improvements:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Direct measurement of cone base diameter using precision calipers before the test
    \item Camera calibration using a reference object of known dimensions in the test section
    \item Multiple shadowgraph views (top and side) to resolve three-dimensional geometry
    \item Surface pressure taps on the cone to measure the actual pressure distribution and validate the Rasmussen approximation
    \item Comparison with computational fluid dynamics (CFD) solutions of the full Navier-Stokes equations
\end{enumerate}

Without these additional measurements, the cause of the anomalous drag discrepancy remains unresolved. The experimental data appear internally consistent (smooth trajectory, constant deceleration, reasonable Mach number), suggesting that the issue may lie in the theoretical predictions rather than the experimental technique. However, definitive conclusions require further investigation beyond the scope of this laboratory exercise.

\section{Summary and Conclusions} \label{sec:summary}

This laboratory experiment successfully demonstrated the use of a Ludwieg tube for supersonic free-flight testing of a conical model. The objectives of characterizing freestream flow conditions, measuring cone geometry, determining experimental drag, and comparing with theoretical predictions were all accomplished, though with unexpected results that highlight important limitations in the experimental methodology.

The freestream flow conditions were calculated from isentropic relations applied to the measured tube pressure and temperature. The analysis yielded a freestream Mach number of $M_\infty = 3.79$ at static pressure $p_\infty = \qty{611}{Pa}$, static temperature $T_\infty = \qty{76.4}{K}$, and density $\rho_\infty = \qty{0.0279}{kg/m^3}$. These conditions are consistent with typical hypersonic wind tunnel operating envelopes and validate the Ludwieg tube's capability to generate well-characterized supersonic flows.

Automated image analysis of 193 shadowgraph frames enabled extraction of the cone geometry and free-flight trajectory. The cone half-angle was measured as $\theta_c = 6.98^\circ$ with a base radius of $r = \qty{4.88}{mm}$. Linear regression fits to the upper and lower cone edges showed excellent consistency across all frames, confirming stable cone orientation during flight. The vertex position versus time exhibited clean quadratic behavior with constant deceleration $a_x = \qty{50.0}{m/s^2}$, corresponding to a drag force of $D = \qty{0.090}{N}$ and drag coefficient of $C_D = 0.196$.

The theoretical inviscid drag prediction using Rasmussen's approximation yielded $C_D = 0.344$, significantly higher than the experimental value. This 43\% discrepancy results in an impossible negative viscous drag coefficient when attempting to decompose the total drag into inviscid and viscous components. Such a result is physically inconsistent and indicates either systematic errors in the experimental measurements (particularly the cone base area determination from two-dimensional projections) or inaccuracies in the theoretical approximations at the tested flow conditions.

Several lessons emerge from this discrepancy:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Importance of dimensional verification:} Pre-test measurement of the cone base diameter with precision instruments would have provided an independent check on the image-based geometry extraction. The current analysis relies entirely on optical measurements subject to perspective distortion.

    \item \textbf{Limitations of analytical approximations:} Rasmussen's formulas are approximate relations derived for slender cones at high Mach numbers. Their accuracy at $M_\infty = 3.79$ and $\theta_c = 7^\circ$ has not been validated. Comparison with exact Taylor-Maccoll solutions or CFD simulations would quantify the approximation error.

    \item \textbf{Value of redundant measurements:} A single shadowgraph view cannot fully resolve three-dimensional geometry. Orthogonal camera views or structured light measurements would eliminate ambiguities in the cone orientation and base dimensions.

    \item \textbf{Need for uncertainty quantification:} The constant-deceleration assumption neglects potential flow unsteadiness, trajectory measurement noise, and systematic errors in timing calibration. Formal uncertainty propagation through the analysis chain would help identify which parameters most strongly influence the final drag coefficient.
\end{enumerate}

Despite these limitations, the experiment successfully demonstrated the fundamental principles of free-flight supersonic testing in a Ludwieg tube environment. The trajectory data exhibited high-quality constant-deceleration behavior, the cone maintained stable orientation throughout flight, and the automated edge detection algorithm performed robustly across nearly 200 frames. The freestream conditions calculated from tube charge measurements were self-consistent and physically reasonable. These positive aspects validate the core experimental technique, even though the drag coefficient comparison revealed discrepancies requiring further investigation.

Future experiments should prioritize direct validation of the cone geometry through pre-test metrology, implementation of CFD simulations to assess the accuracy of the Rasmussen approximations, and expansion to a wider range of cone angles and Mach numbers to systematically characterize the measurement uncertainties. With these enhancements, the Ludwieg tube free-flight technique offers a powerful capability for rapid, low-cost hypersonic aerodynamic testing that complements traditional force balance measurements and computational methods.
